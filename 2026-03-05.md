## SVT Review

### AVNRT (AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia) — Revisited
- Dual AV nodal pathway physiology: fast pathway (short refractory, fast conduction), slow pathway (longer refractory, slow conduction)
- Typical AVNRT: slow-fast circuit (antegrade slow, retrograde fast)
  - Retrograde P wave buried in or at the tail of QRS
  - Classic sign: **pseudo R' in V1** (retrograde P creates a small terminal positive deflection)
  - **Short RP < 70 ms**
- Atypical AVNRT (fast-slow): long RP tachycardia

### Orthodromic AVRT
- Reentry using AV node antegrade + accessory pathway retrograde
- Narrow QRS (His-Purkinje system used for antegrade)
- Retrograde P waves visible after the QRS in the ST segment
- Short RP (but longer than AVNRT)

### Atrial Tachycardia (AT)
- Third most common SVT
- Focal automaticity from ectopic atrial site
- P-wave morphology different from sinus (usually negative in inferior leads if low atrial focus)
- **Long RP > PR** — P wave is well separated from preceding QRS
- AV node not required for tachycardia maintenance
- **Does not terminate with adenosine** (unlike AVNRT and AVRT) — adenosine may cause transient AV block, revealing continuing atrial tachycardia
- Causes: atrial scarring/fibrosis, structural heart disease, drug toxicity, thyrotoxicosis
- Crista terminalis and pulmonary vein ostia are common sites of origin

## Fascicular (Hemi) Blocks

Blocks in one fascicle of the left bundle branch — QRS < 120 ms (unlike full BBB)

### Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB / LAHB)
- Supplies anterior/superior LV
- Block → **left axis deviation** (≥ −45°, often −60° to −90°)
- QRS slightly prolonged (100–110 ms) but < 120 ms
- Small q in I, AVL; small r in II, III, AVF
- Most common fascicular block; common with age

### Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB / LPHB)
- Supplies inferior/posterior LV
- Block → **right axis deviation** (+120° or more)
- Must exclude other causes of right axis deviation (RVH, PE, dextrocardia)
- Less common than LAFB (posterior fascicle has dual blood supply)

## Key EKG Intervals

| Interval | Normal Range          | Significance                                     |
|----------|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| PR       | 120–200 ms            | AV conduction time; >200 ms = 1st degree block   |
| QRS      | 80–120 ms             | Ventricular conduction; >120 ms = BBB            |
| QT       | ≤440 ms (corrected)   | Ventricular repolarization; prolongation = arrhythmia risk |
| QTc      | QT / √(RR in seconds) | Bazett's formula for rate correction             |

### QT Prolongation
- QTc > 440 ms (men), > 460 ms (women) = prolonged
- Risk: torsades de pointes (polymorphic VT)
- Causes: congenital long QT syndrome, drugs (antiarrhythmics, antipsychotics, antibiotics, antifungals), hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia
- The case of the woman with long QT (QTc ~600 ms): 2:1 AV block from long repolarization; proceeded to torsades during a procedure

## Chamber Enlargement

### Right Atrial Enlargement (RAE)
- **P pulmonale**: tall, peaked P wave > 2.5 mm in lead II
- Causes: COPD, pulmonary hypertension, RV pressure overload
- P-wave axis usually rightward

### Left Atrial Enlargement (LAE)
- **P mitrale**: wide, notched (bifid) P wave > 120 ms in lead II; looks like an "M"
- **V1**: large negative terminal component (ptfV1 ≤ −1 mm × 40 ms area)
- Causes: mitral stenosis/regurgitation, LV diastolic dysfunction, hypertension

### Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)
- **Sokolow-Lyon criteria**: S in V1 + R in V5 or V6 > 35 mm (3.5 mV)
- **aVL-based voltage criterion**: tall R wave in aVL (supportive of LVH)
- Often accompanied by LV strain pattern: down-sloping ST depression + T-wave inversion in lateral leads (I, AVL, V5, V6)
- Causes: hypertension (most common), aortic stenosis, HCM

### Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)
- Right axis deviation (> +90°)
- R > S in V1 (dominant R wave in V1)
- Deep S in V5, V6
- Often right atrial enlargement as well
- Causes: pulmonary hypertension, pulmonic stenosis, congenital heart disease

## Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW)

### Mechanism
- **Accessory pathway (Kent bundle)**: muscle connection between atrium and ventricle outside the AV node
- **Non-decremental conduction**: accessory pathway does not slow conduction like the AV node → antegrade pre-excitation during sinus rhythm
- **Delta wave**: slurring of initial QRS upstroke (early ventricular activation via accessory pathway)
- **Short PR interval** (< 120 ms): atrial impulse bypasses AV delay
- **Widened QRS** (due to delta wave adding to the beginning of QRS)

### Risk of Pre-Excited AF
- If AF develops and conducts antegrade down the accessory pathway:
  - **Pre-excited AF**: very rapid, irregular, wide-complex tachycardia
  - R-R intervals as short as < 230 ms = high risk for VF
  - Extremely dangerous — can degenerate to VF
- **Never give AV nodal blockers** (adenosine, beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin) in pre-excited AF → blocks AV node but not accessory pathway → exclusive conduction down accessory pathway → VF
- Treatment: IV procainamide or ibutilide; electrical cardioversion if unstable

### Ablation
- **Curative procedure**: radiofrequency or cryoablation of accessory pathway
- Access: transseptal (left-sided pathways) or retrograde aortic approach
- Procedure locates pathway using electrophysiology study (EPS)
- Risk of injury near coronary arteries or natural conduction system (rare)
- Success rate > 95% with experienced operator

## Ablation Procedure (General)

1. Venous/arterial access: femoral veins (±femoral artery), subclavian veins
2. Fluoroscopic or 3D mapping to navigate catheters to heart
3. Diagnostic catheters placed in RA, coronary sinus, RV, HB region
4. 3D electroanatomic mapping system creates geometry and activation map
5. Identify arrhythmia mechanism by programmed stimulation
6. Deliver radiofrequency (RF) energy: thermal ablation (~50–70°C, 30–90s)
   - OR cryoablation (freeze to –70°C)
   - OR pulsed field ablation (PFA, electroporation — newer)
7. Complications: cardiac perforation/tamponade (0.1–1%), stroke (0.1%), AV block, vascular access complications

## Adenosine Response Diagnostic Table

| Arrhythmia           | Response to Adenosine                          |
|----------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| AVNRT                | Terminates (breaks reentry through AV node)    |
| Orthodromic AVRT     | Terminates (AV node needed for circuit)        |
| Atrial tachycardia   | AV block without termination; AT continues     |
| Atrial flutter       | AV block → flutter waves revealed; flutter continues |
| Sinus tachycardia    | Transient slowing; gradually speeds up again   |
| VT (most forms)      | Does not terminate (no AV node in circuit)     |

## Atrial Flutter

### Mechanism
- **Macroreentry** around the tricuspid valve (cavotricuspid isthmus, CTI)
- Typical (counterclockwise flutter): activation goes around the RA counterclockwise when viewed from the front
- **Atrial rate**: ~300 bpm (range 250–350)
- **Sawtooth pattern**: negative flutter waves (F waves) in inferior leads (II, III, AVF)

### Ventricular Rates
- Usually **2:1 block** → ventricular rate ≈ 150 bpm (most common presentation)
- Can be 3:1 (100 bpm) or 4:1 (75 bpm) depending on AV nodal conduction
- Rate changes (2:1 → 3:1 → 4:1) occur spontaneously or with AV nodal blocking agents
- Irregular response possible if variable block

### CTI Ablation
- Creates a line of block across the cavotricuspid isthmus
- Cures typical atrial flutter in > 95% of cases
- Bidirectional block across CTI confirmed by pacing

## Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia (MAT)

- **≥ 3 distinctly different P-wave morphologies** with varying PR intervals
- Rate > 100 bpm (if < 100, called wandering atrial pacemaker)
- Irregular rhythm (may mimic AF)
- **Mechanism**: multiple foci in the atria firing automatically
- **Causes**: severe COPD, electrolyte abnormalities, sepsis, theophylline toxicity
- **Treatment**: treat underlying illness; magnesium, calcium channel blockers; NOT cardioversion (structural problem)

---

## Wandering Atrial Pacemaker

- ≥ 3 different P-wave morphologies but **rate < 100 bpm**
- Usually in older patients with atrial disease or high vagal tone
- Benign; no specific treatment needed
