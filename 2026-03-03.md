## Anatomy of the Cardiac Conduction System

- **SA node**: Epicardial structure at junction of SVC and RA; main pacemaker (60–100 bpm)
- **Crista terminalis**: Ridge of muscle in RA; site of subsidiary atrial pacemakers; all cells along the crista can develop automaticity
- **Bachmann's bundle**: Interatrial conduction pathway from RA to LA
- **AV node**: In the interatrial septum near the coronary sinus os; slows conduction (~100–120 ms AV delay)
- **Bundle of His**: Penetrates the fibrous skeleton of the heart; divides into right and left bundle branches
- **Left bundle branch**: Further divides into:
  - **Left anterior fascicle (LAF)**: Supplies anterior/superior LV
  - **Left posterior fascicle (LPF)**: Supplies inferior/posterior LV
  - (Possible septal fascicle — debated)
- **Right bundle branch**: Travels down the interventricular septum → moderator band → RV free wall
- **Purkinje network**: Terminal subendocardial network for fast, simultaneous ventricular activation

## Clinical EKG Reading System

Dr. Tatro's systematic approach (always follow this order):

1. **Rate** — calculate from R-R intervals
2. **Rhythm** — regular or irregular? Type?
3. **Axis** — frontal plane mean QRS axis
4. **Intervals** — PR, QRS duration, QT/QTc
5. **Atrial enlargement** — P-wave morphology
6. **Ventricular hypertrophy** — voltage criteria

### EKG Paper
- Paper speed: 25 mm/s
- Small box = 1 mm = **40 ms**
- Large box = 5 mm = **200 ms**

---

## Heart Rate Calculation

Using R-R interval (number of large boxes):
- 1 box → 300 bpm
- 2 boxes → 150 bpm
- 3 boxes → 100 bpm
- 4 boxes → 75 bpm
- 5 boxes → 60 bpm
- 6 boxes → 50 bpm

For irregular rhythms: count complexes in 6-second strip × 10.

## Heart Blocks

### First-Degree AV Block
- **PR interval > 200 ms** (> 1 large box)
- Every P wave conducts; no dropped beats
- Often benign; seen with high vagal tone, inferior MI, AV nodal disease, medications (β-blockers, CCBs, digoxin)
- No treatment necessary in isolation

### Second-Degree AV Block — Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach)
- **Progressive PR interval lengthening** with each beat
- Eventually one P wave **fails to conduct** (dropped QRS)
- After the dropped beat, PR interval resets to its shortest value
- Block typically located **at the AV node**
- Often benign; can occur in athletes, sleep, inferior MI
- Ladder diagram: lines become progressively more slanted, then one line does not reach the ventricle

### Second-Degree AV Block — Mobitz Type II
- **Constant PR interval** followed by sudden dropped QRS (no warning)
- Block located **below** the AV node (bundle of His or bundle branches)
- More serious: often progresses to complete heart block
- Requires permanent pacemaker evaluation
- QRS may be wide (suggests infranodal/bundle branch level block)
- Ladder diagram: constant slope lines, then one line suddenly stops

### Third-Degree (Complete) Heart Block
- **Complete AV dissociation**: P waves and QRS complexes march at independent rates
- P wave rate > QRS rate
- Ventricular escape rhythm takes over at 20–40 bpm (wide, bizarre QRS from ventricular escape) or 40–60 bpm (narrow if junctional escape)
- **Urgent pacemaker indication** — risk of asystole and sudden death
- Causes: inferior MI (AV nodal), anterior MI or degenerative disease (infranodal), Lyme disease, drug toxicity
- Ladder diagram: atrial and ventricular lines march independently with no connections

## Junctional Rhythm

- Escape rhythm arising from AV junction (just below AV node)
- Rate: 40–60 bpm
- **Narrow QRS** (conducts normally through the His-Purkinje system)
- **Retrograde P waves**: inverted in inferior leads (II, III, AVF); usually falls within or just after the QRS
- Occurs when SA node fails or is overdrive-suppressed
- Common with high vagal tone, sick sinus syndrome, or digitalis toxicity

### Pacemaker Syndrome
- When atrium contracts against a closed tricuspid valve (e.g., junctional rhythm or VVI pacing)
- Elevated cannon A waves in jugular venous pulsation
- Symptoms: pulsations in neck/chest, lightheadedness from baroreceptor reflex activation
- Historical reason for the name: first pacemakers only paced the ventricle

## Premature Atrial Contractions (PACs / APCs)

- Extra beat originating from ectopic atrial focus (outside SA node)
- **Different P-wave morphology** from sinus P waves
- Normal or slightly aberrant QRS (if conducted with partial bundle branch block)
- **Blocked PAC**: ectopic atrial beat occurs so early that AV node is still refractory → no QRS; may mimic sinus pause
- Common in normal people; increase with caffeine, alcohol, stress, age
- Wandering atrial pacemaker: ≥3 different P-wave morphologies at normal rate; benign variant in older patients

## Precordial Lead Anatomy

- V1–V2: overlying right ventricle (right sternal border)
- V3: AV groove region (anterior)
- V4: apex region
- V5–V6: lateral left ventricle

### 12-Lead EKG Groupings (Coronary Territory)
- **Inferior leads**: II, III, AVF → inferior wall (RCA territory)
- **Lateral leads**: I, AVL, V5, V6 → lateral wall (LCx territory)
- **Anterior leads**: V1–V4 → anterior wall (LAD territory)
- **Basal/septal**: V1–V2 (posterior descending reciprocal changes)

## QRS Nomenclature

- **Q wave**: initial negative deflection (before first positive deflection)
- **R wave**: first positive deflection
- **S wave**: negative deflection after an R wave
- **R' (R prime)**: second positive deflection
- Capitalization indicates size: large = capital (R, S, Q); small = lowercase (r, s, q)

### Normal R-Wave Progression in Precordial Leads

- V1: rS (small r, large S)
- V2–V3: transitional
- V4: RS (transition zone; R ≈ S)
- V5–V6: qR (tall R, small s)

**Poor R-wave progression**: loss of R waves (flat or decreasing R across precordium) → anterior MI, LVH, LBBB

## Bundle Branch Blocks (Introduction)

### Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)
- QRS ≥ 120 ms
- **RSR' pattern ("bunny ears")** in V1 — the late R' represents delayed RV activation from LV spreading transseptally
- Broad S wave in V5, V6, lead I

### Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)
- QRS ≥ 120 ms
- **QS pattern** (or broad notched negative QRS) in V1
- **Tall, monophasic R wave** in V6 (broad, notched, or M-shaped)
- Alters axis interpretation — cannot diagnose ischemia/hypertrophy in presence of LBBB

## AVNRT (AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia)

- Most common form of SVT in adults
- **Mechanism**: Dual AV nodal pathways (fast and slow) with different refractory periods
  - Antegrade conduction over slow pathway, retrograde over fast pathway (slow-fast = typical AVNRT, ~90%)
  - P waves buried within or just after the QRS
- **EKG findings**: Narrow complex, regular tachycardia at 150–250 bpm; **pseudo R' in V1** (retrograde P hidden at end of QRS); short RP interval (RP < PR)
- Termination: vagal maneuvers (Valsalva, carotid sinus massage), IV adenosine

---

## AVRT (AV Reentrant Tachycardia) — Introduction

- Mechanism: accessory pathway (bypass tract) creating a reentry circuit using the AV node in one direction and the accessory pathway in the other
- **Orthodromic AVRT**: antegrade through AV node, retrograde through accessory pathway → narrow QRS (most common form)
- **Antidromic AVRT**: antegrade through accessory pathway, retrograde through AV node → wide QRS (mimics VT)
- RP relationship: short RP (retrograde P close to QRS)

---

## SVT Classification by RP Interval

| Category       | RP Interval    | Common Diagnoses                        |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Short RP       | RP < PR        | AVNRT (typical), orthodromic AVRT       |
| Long RP        | RP > PR        | Atrial tachycardia, atypical AVNRT, PJRT|
| Very long RP   | P near next QRS| Sinus tachycardia (physiologic)         |

## Ladder Diagrams in Practice

Three tiers: A (atria), AV junction, V (ventricles)
- Normal: each diagonal line from A to V with steady PR delay
- Wenckebach: progressively steeper lines until one stops at AV tier
- Mobitz II: constant angle until sudden stop
- Complete heart block: two separate series of lines, A and V independent
- Junctional rhythm: lines emerge from AV junction going both up (retrograde P) and down (QRS) simultaneously
