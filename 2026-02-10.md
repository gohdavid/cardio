## Overview

Quantitative model of the peripheral circulation: mean systemic (circulatory) filling pressure, the venous return curve, vascular waterfall phenomenon in collapsible tubes, and the Windkessel model of arterial compliance.

---

## Starling Forces and Edema

- Exchange at capillaries governed by Starling forces:
  - **Filtration forces**: hydrostatic pressure in capillary, oncotic pressure in interstitium
  - **Reabsorption forces**: oncotic pressure in plasma, hydrostatic pressure in interstitium
- Net filtration pressure = (Pc – Pi) – σ(πc – πi)
- Edema results when filtration exceeds lymphatic drainage:
  - Elevated venous pressure (heart failure) → ↑Pc
  - Hypoalbuminemia → ↓πc
  - Increased capillary permeability (inflammation, sepsis)
  - Lymphatic obstruction

## Arterial and Venous Compliance

- Arterial compliance (Ca) ≈ **2 mL/mmHg** — arteries are stiff, serve as pressure reservoir
- Venous compliance (Cv) ≈ **100 mL/mmHg** — veins are ~50× more compliant, serve as volume reservoir
- ~70% of total blood volume resides in the venous system
- Venous tone (sympathetic venoconstriction) decreases effective Cv, mobilizing blood centrally

## Peripheral Circuit Model

The peripheral circulation is modeled as:
- Arterial compartment (Ca, high pressure, ~100 mmHg)
- Venous compartment (Cv, low pressure, ~5–10 mmHg)
- Connecting resistance (arterioles and capillaries, Ra)
- Heart pump connecting venous inlet to arterial outlet

## Mean Systemic (Circulatory) Filling Pressure (PMS)

> **PMS = Total blood volume in descending compartments / Total compliance**

- PMS is the equilibrium pressure when the heart stops (zero flow state)
- Reflects the "stressed volume" — the volume above the unstressed volume in veins and arteries
- Normal PMS ≈ **7–10 mmHg**
- Key determinants:
  - ↑Blood volume → ↑PMS (proportional)
  - ↑Venous tone (sympathetic, venoconstriction) → ↓effective compliance → ↑PMS
  - Hemorrhage → ↓PMS

**Doubling blood volume → doubles PMS** (linear relationship with stressed volume)

## Venous Return Curve

The venous return curve plots venous return (VR) on the y-axis vs. right atrial pressure (Pf) on the x-axis:

> **VR = (PMS – Pf) / RVR**

Key features:
- **At Pf = PMS**: VR = 0 (no gradient to drive flow back to heart)
- **As Pf decreases below PMS**: VR increases linearly
- **At Pf ≤ 0 mmHg**: VR plateaus — the vascular waterfall limit
- Slope = –1 / total resistance of the circuit
- x-intercept = PMS (the mean systemic filling pressure)

## Vascular Waterfall (Flow Limitation in Collapsible Tubes)

- When right atrial pressure falls to ~0 mmHg, the large veins entering the thorax collapse under the atmospheric external pressure
- Further reduction of Pf cannot increase VR — the flow is "limited" by the collapsible segment
- This creates a **plateau** on the venous return curve at low Pf values
- Analogous to a waterfall: increasing the drop below the falls does not increase flow rate
- Physiological example: during forced deep inspiration, great veins collapse at the thoracic inlet

## Manipulations of the Venous Return Curve

| Intervention              | Effect on PMS | Effect on Slope | Effect on VR curve        |
|---------------------------|---------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Blood transfusion         | ↑             | No change       | Shifts right (parallel)   |
| Hemorrhage                | ↓             | No change       | Shifts left (parallel)    |
| Sympathetic stimulation   | ↑             | Shallower if resistance to venous return rises | Shifts right; slope depends on the balance of venoconstriction vs. arteriolar constriction |
| Vasodilation              | Usually little direct change in PMS | Steeper if resistance falls | Rotates upward about the x-intercept if PMS is unchanged |
| Arteriovenous fistula     | Usually little direct change in PMS | Steeper (R↓) | Rotates upward; higher VR and CO at a given Pf |

## Windkessel Model

The Windkessel (German: "air chamber") model represents the arterial system as a compliant reservoir:
- Heart injects blood in pulses
- Arterial compliance stores energy during systole → releases it during diastole
- This smooths pulsatile flow into more continuous peripheral flow
- Two-element Windkessel: compliance (Ca) + peripheral resistance (R)
- Three-element adds aortic impedance (characteristic impedance Zc) for better high-frequency accuracy

**Key insight**: Pulse pressure (PP = systolic – diastolic) is inversely related to arterial compliance:
- ↓Ca (stiff arteries, aging) → ↑PP
- ↑Ca (young, elastic arteries) → ↓PP

## Clinical Pearls

- PMS is the "set point" for venous return; interventions that raise PMS (blood transfusion, venoconstriction) improve VR and CO in hypovolemic states.
- Aortic stiffening (age, hypertension) → high pulse pressure → isolated systolic hypertension.
- In tension pneumothorax, elevated intrathoracic pressure collapses the great veins → obstructive shock.
