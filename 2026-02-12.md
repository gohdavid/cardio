## The Cardiac Cycle: Phases

### Left Ventricular Phases (pressure-time curve)

1. **Isovolumetric Contraction (IVC)**
   - Both mitral and aortic valves closed
   - Pressure rises sharply with no volume change
   - Duration ~50–80 ms

2. **Rapid Ejection**
   - Aortic valve opens when LV pressure exceeds aortic diastolic pressure (~80 mmHg)
   - Most of SV ejected in first third of systole

3. **Reduced Ejection**
   - LV pressure begins to fall; aortic valve remains open
   - Pressure in LV and aorta track each other closely

4. **Isovolumetric Relaxation (IVR)**
   - Aortic valve closes (dicrotic notch on aortic pressure trace)
   - LV pressure falls rapidly, volume constant until mitral valve opens

5. **Rapid Ventricular Filling**
   - Mitral valve opens when LV pressure < LA pressure
   - ~80% of filling occurs passively in early diastole

6. **Slow Filling (Diastasis)**
   - Gradual equalization of LV and LA pressures

7. **Atrial Systole (Atrial Kick)**
   - Atrial contraction contributes ~20% of EDV at rest; more with elevated heart rate or impaired LV compliance

### Heart Sounds
- **S1**: mitral (and tricuspid) valve closure — beginning of systole
- **S2**: aortic (and pulmonic) valve closure — beginning of diastole
- **S3**: early diastolic sound, rapid ventricular filling in dilated/failing ventricle
- **S4**: late diastolic, stiff/non-compliant ventricle resisting atrial kick

## Pressure-Volume (PV) Loop

The PV loop is a plot of LV pressure (y-axis) vs. LV volume (x-axis) traversed during one cardiac cycle:

```
      Pressure
         |         ESPVR (slope = Emax = contractility)
         |        /
    120 -|-------*---------*
         |      /           \
         |     /    Ejection  \
         |    *    (systole)   *---> dicrotic notch area
         |    |                |
         |    * Filling         *
         |    (diastole)        |
     10 -|----*-----------*----
         |    EDV          ESV
         +----+----+----+----> Volume
```

Key points on the loop:
- **A → B**: Isovolumetric contraction (vertical rise, bottom-right)
- **B → C**: Ejection (moves left along systolic curve)
- **C → D**: Isovolumetric relaxation (vertical fall, top-left)
- **D → A**: Filling (moves right along diastolic curve)

### Derived Quantities from PV Loop

- **Stroke Volume (SV)** = EDV – ESV (width of loop)
- **Ejection Fraction (EF)** = SV / EDV × 100%
  - Normal EF: **55–65%**
  - Reduced EF heart failure: EF < 40%
  - "Preserved" (HFpEF): EF > 50% but diastolic dysfunction
- **Stroke Work (SW)** = area enclosed within PV loop

## End-Systolic Pressure-Volume Relationship (ESPVR)

- The upper-left corner of each PV loop (end-systole) lies on a straight line called the ESPVR.
- **Slope of ESPVR = Emax** = end-systolic elastance = **contractility index**
- ESPVR is relatively load-independent — it characterizes intrinsic myocardial function.
- Positive inotropy → steeper ESPVR slope (higher Emax) + leftward shift
- Negative inotropy → shallower slope + rightward shift

---

## Frank-Starling Mechanism on the PV Loop

- Increasing preload (higher EDV): loop shifts right; SV increases; EF may be maintained
- Decreasing preload: loop shifts left; SV decreases
- The length-tension relationship of the sarcomere is the underlying mechanism

---

## Effect of Afterload

- Increasing afterload (higher aortic pressure): loop becomes taller/narrower; SV decreases; ESV increases
- Decreasing afterload (vasodilation): wider loop; SV increases
- At a given contractility (fixed ESPVR), SV is determined by the intersection of ESPVR with the end-systolic point

## Excitation-Contraction Coupling

1. Action potential arrives → voltage-gated L-type Ca²⁺ channels (DHP receptors) open in T-tubules
2. Small Ca²⁺ influx triggers massive Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) via ryanodine receptors (RyR2) — **calcium-induced calcium release (CICR)**
3. Ca²⁺ binds troponin C → removes tropomyosin inhibition → actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling → contraction
4. Relaxation: Ca²⁺ removed by:
   - SERCA pump (SR Ca²⁺-ATPase) → back into SR (rate-limiting for diastolic relaxation)
   - Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) → out of cell
   - Sarcolemmal Ca²⁺-ATPase → out of cell

### Key Points
- Force of contraction ∝ intracellular Ca²⁺ availability
- Sympathetic stimulation (β₁): ↑L-type channel opening (positive chronotropy and inotropy) + phosphorylates phospholamban → ↑SERCA activity → faster relaxation (positive lusitropy)
- Digitalis: inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑intracellular Na⁺ → ↓NCX activity → ↑intracellular Ca²⁺ → increased contractility

## Sarcomere Physiology

- Optimal sarcomere length: ~2.2 μm (maximum actin-myosin overlap)
- At too-short lengths: filaments overlap reduces force
- At too-long lengths: reduced cross-bridge formation (overstretched)
- In the heart, operating range is 1.8–2.2 μm at normal loading
- The rising limb of the Frank-Starling curve corresponds to sarcomeres in the range below optimal length

## Key Equations

| Quantity           | Formula                          |
|--------------------|----------------------------------|
| Ejection fraction  | EF = SV / EDV                    |
| Stroke volume      | SV = EDV – ESV                   |
| Contractility      | Emax = ΔPESP / ΔVESP (ESPVR slope)|
| Stroke work        | Area enclosed in PV loop         |

## Clinical Pearls

- Acute MI: ischemic segment drops off ESPVR; loop becomes narrow, SV falls, ESV rises.
- Dilated cardiomyopathy: flat ESPVR, very wide/low loop, very high EDV, low EF.
- Aortic stenosis: tall narrow loop (high afterload); hypertrophy normalizes wall stress but diastolic dysfunction often develops.
- Mitral regurgitation: volume ejected into low-pressure LA → wide loop, high SV, but effective forward flow is lower than measured by Fick.
