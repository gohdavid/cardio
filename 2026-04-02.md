## Heart-Lung Pumping Unit — Review

### Anatomy (Detailed Review)
- RA → RV (tricuspid valve) → pulmonary artery (pulmonic valve) → lungs → pulmonary veins
- No valve between pulmonary veins and LA
- LA → LV (mitral valve) → aorta (aortic valve) → peripheral circulation

### Two-Spring (Two-State) RV Model
- **Diastolic curve** (relaxed state): actin and myosin are not bound; spring is relaxed
- **Systolic curve** (active state): actin and myosin are cross-bridged; spring is tense
- Volume (x-axis) = stretching of the spring (increases tension/pressure)
- This is the Hooke's law relationship applied to the ventricle
- Four phases: filling → isovolumetric contraction → ejection → relaxation

### Cardiac Output from the Cardiac Cycle

> **CO = f × SV** = f × (VEDV – VESV)

- f = heart rate (beats per minute)
- SV = EDV – ESV (stroke volume from PV loop)
- Assumptions for this simplified relationship to hold:
  1. Pulmonary arterial pressure approximately constant
  2. Right ventricle not maximally dilated (operating in Region 1)
  3. Left ventricle not failing (aortic pressure < max LV systolic pressure)

**The right ventricle is the rate-limiting pump** (like the "third grader" vs. LV as "bodybuilder"):
- Normal CO is determined by RV performance, not LV
- LV can handle any load within normal range; RV is the weaker link

## Three Regions of Cardiac Output (Review)

| Region | Description                          | Conditions                            |
|--------|--------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| I      | RV-limited; CO increases with VR     | Normal physiology (RV happy, LV happy)|
| II     | RV maximally dilated; CO plateaus    | Very high filling pressures (RV saturated) |
| III    | LV failing; CO limited by LV         | Aortic pressure > max LV capacity     |

- **All normal clinical physiology is in Region I**
- Region II and III encountered in critical illness or cardiomyopathy

## Venous Return Curve and Cardiac Output Curve — Mathematical Summary

### Venous Return Curve
- Q_VR = (PMS – Pf) / R_total
- Plot: VR (y) vs. Pf (x); slope = –1/R_total; x-intercept = PMS
- Plateau at Pf ≤ 0 (vascular waterfall)

### Cardiac Output Curve (Simplified)
- Slope determined by heart rate and contractility
- Maximum value: Vmax / Cd (RV maximum volume over pulmonary compliance)
- Flat region at high filling pressures (RV saturated)

### Steady State
- At steady state: Q_CO = Q_VR → this determines the operating point (intersection)
- Perturbations:
  - ↑PMS (sympathetic stimulation, blood transfusion): VR curve shifts right
  - ↑Resistance to venous return: VR curve slope becomes shallower
  - ↑HR or contractility: CO curve slope steeper / shifts up

### Resistance Does Not Appear in the Cardiac Output Equation
- Total peripheral resistance changes the slope of the VR curve
- Does NOT directly appear in the simplified cardiac output equation (CO = f × SV at steady state)
- But it influences the steady-state CO by changing where the VR curve intersects the CO curve

## ECG Theory Review

### Single Equivalent Moving Dipole (Review)

**Core principle**: Each myocyte carries a local dipole. The sum of all dipoles at any instant = net heart dipole vector **m(t)**.

> **φ = (m · cos θ) / (4πσr²)**

- Each EKG lead = projection of m onto the lead axis
- The EKG is a time-varying plot of these projections as m rotates through the cardiac cycle

### Experimental Visualization
- Conceptual "zero-Kelvin bath" experiment:
  - At any time t, freeze the heart → measure dipole at each myocyte → sum = net vector
  - Restart heart; repeat at t + Δt
  - Plot the changing projection over time = EKG waveform

### How to Predict Any Lead
1. Know the path of depolarization
2. At each instant, determine the net dipole direction and magnitude
3. Project onto the lead axis vector
4. The sign and amplitude of the projection = the EKG deflection at that instant

## Normal Depolarization Sequence and Lead Correlates

### P Wave (Atrial Depolarization)
- Starts at SA node (epicardial junction of SVC/RA)
- Spreads left, inferiorly, and slightly anteriorly
- Net vector: positive in lead II; biphasic in V1 (right atrial component positive, left atrial component negative)

### QRS (Ventricular Depolarization)
1. **Septal depolarization** (left bundle fires first): left → right across the septum
   - Creates initial positive deflection in V1 (small r wave)
   - Creates initial negative deflection in lateral leads (q wave in V5/V6/lead I)
2. **Main LV activation**: enormous leftward vector (LV mass dominates)
   - Creates main negative deflection in V1 (S wave)
   - Creates tall positive R wave in lateral leads
3. **Basal LV completion**: posteriorly directed vector
   - Creates terminal small negative deflection in some leads

### T Wave (Ventricular Repolarization)
- Repolarization proceeds in **opposite direction to depolarization** (epicardium before endocardium — epicardial APD shorter)
- Net vector aligns **same direction as QRS**
- Therefore T wave is upright in leads where QRS is upright
- V1: QRS mainly negative → T wave also inverted (or flat) in V1 is normal

### Linear Dependence of Leads
- 6 limb leads are NOT independent: only 2 are mathematically independent
- The other 4 are linear combinations
- AVR ≈ inverse of the average of lead I and lead II
- This is why the same physical process looks different (but predictably related) in each lead

## Chest (Precordial) Leads

- Chest leads provide information about the heart dipole in the **horizontal plane** (perpendicular to frontal plane)
- Cannot be derived from limb leads alone (different plane)
- Each precordial lead = projection from heart to that specific electrode location
- V1: anterior/rightward → sees RV, septum
- V6: lateral/leftward → sees lateral LV
- Normal P wave in V1: biphasic (right atrial depolarization toward V1 → positive; left atrial depolarization away from V1 → negative)

## Arrhythmia Review for Exam

### Atrial Fibrillation
- **Irregularly irregular** rhythm
- **No P waves** (all myocytes depolarizing independently → no concerted dipole → no P wave)
- Mechanism: many atrial myocytes bombard the AV node with irregular impulses; the AV node conducts a variable subset of them → irregular ventricular response
- Fast rate (typically 100–180 bpm uncontrolled)
- Predictability of next beat: **none** — completely random spacing

### Atrial Flutter
- **Regular** rhythm (macroreentry circuit)
- **Sawtooth P waves** (flutter waves): inverted in inferior leads, ~300/min
- **Concerted** atrial activation (all turning around the tricuspid annulus together) → regular, organized P waves
- Ventricular rate = atrial rate / n (2:1 at 150 bpm most common)
- P waves are inverted in II, III, AVF (activation going superiorly around the circuit)

### Ventricular Tachycardia
- Rate ≥ 100 bpm
- **Wide QRS** (outside normal conduction system; slow cell-to-cell myocardial conduction)
- May or may not have visible P waves (VA dissociation)
- When P waves visible: they are **dissociated** (march at different rate than QRS)
- Proof of VT: VA dissociation, capture beats, fusion beats
- Most VT arises from LV (more myocardium, more disease)

### Heart Blocks
- **1st degree**: PR > 200 ms; every P wave conducts; no dropped beats
- **2nd degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach)**: progressive PR lengthening → dropped beat → resets
- **2nd degree Mobitz II**: constant PR → sudden dropped beat; infranodal; requires pacemaker
- **3rd degree (complete)**: P waves and QRS completely independent; atrial rate > ventricular rate
