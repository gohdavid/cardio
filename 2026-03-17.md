## Premature Ventricular Contractions (PVCs)

### Characteristics
- **Wide, bizarre QRS** with no preceding P wave
- **Full compensatory pause**: sinus node continues at its rate; the next sinus beat falls exactly on schedule because the SA node is not reset by the PVC
- Very common — occurs in all healthy individuals at some point

### Hemodynamic Effect of PVCs
- PVC contraction is **less efficient** (asynchronous activation via myocardium, not His-Purkinje)
- PVC itself may generate **little or no pulse** (ventricle contracting too soon after previous beat; reduced filling time)
- **Post-PVC beat**: enhanced contractility (post-extrasystolic potentiation) — larger than normal pulse

### Pseudo-Bradycardia
- In **ventricular bigeminy** (every other beat a PVC), the PVC may not generate a palpable pulse
- Pulse oximeter or manual pulse check shows rate of 30–40 bpm (only sensing sinus beats)
- EKG shows rate of 60–80 bpm (alternating sinus + PVC)
- Key: treat the PVCs, not the "bradycardia"

### PVC Morphology and Origin
- **Monomorphic PVCs**: all look the same → single focal origin
- **Multifocal PVCs**: different morphologies → multiple origins; not necessarily more dangerous but noted
- **Origin determination**:
  - **V1 negative (LBBB morphology)**: PVC from RV (activation moves away from V1)
  - **V1 positive (RBBB morphology)**: PVC from LV (activation moves toward V1)
  - **Inferior axis (positive II, III, AVF)**: "Inferiorly directed" → origin at outflow tract (high in heart, activation goes downward)
  - **Superior axis (negative II, III, AVF)**: "Superiorly directed" → apical or inferior origin

### Common PVC Sites
- **Outflow tract** (most common in structurally normal hearts): RVOT (LBBB morphology, inferior axis), LVOT (RBBB morphology, inferior axis)
- **Papillary muscles**: RBBB morphology, variable axis
- **Mitral annulus**: lateral and posterior LV
- "Lost" pacemaker cells from development → transitional cells around valve annuli are the substrate for most idiopathic PVCs

### Clinical Significance
- **Benign** in structurally normal heart: if symptoms bother patient → beta-blockers, CCBs, or ablation
- **PVC burden > 20%**: risk of PVC-induced cardiomyopathy (reversible with treatment)
- **Rare PVCs from scarred Purkinje tissue**: may trigger dangerous arrhythmias (especially post-MI or in cardiomyopathy)

## Ventricular Bigeminy and Trigeminy

- **Bigeminy**: every other beat is a PVC (sinus–PVC–sinus–PVC pattern)
- **Trigeminy**: every third beat is a PVC
- Arterial line shows low-amplitude waveform for PVC beats (poor hemodynamics during the PVC)
- Pseudo-bradycardia from pulse counting only sinus beats

## Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)

- **Rate < 100 bpm**, wide complex, regular
- Commonly post-reperfusion (after coronary intervention); benign
- Often retrograde P wave buried in ST segment
- No treatment required; self-limited
- **Not VT** by definition (VT = rate ≥ 100 bpm)

---

## Monomorphic Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

- **Rate ≥ 100 bpm**, wide QRS (> 120 ms), all beats look the same
- Regular (distinguishes from polymorphic VT or VF)
- **Non-sustained VT**: < 30 seconds; may be benign or a warning
- **Sustained VT**: ≥ 30 seconds; always requires evaluation

### VT with Normal Heart (Idiopathic VT)
- Female predominance (2:1)
- Usually from RVOT or LVOT (same locations as common PVCs)
- Benign prognosis; can cause symptoms, lightheadedness, syncope
- **2:1 female predominance**; may worsen with pregnancy or menstrual cycle
- Treatment: beta-blockers, CCBs, or catheter ablation (very effective, > 90% success)

### VT with Structural Heart Disease
- Most common cause: **old myocardial infarction** (healed scar)
- Mechanism: **scar-related reentry** — islands of surviving myocytes within fibrotic scar create slow conduction corridors
  - Two areas of conduction block
  - One corridor with slow conduction creates a reentry loop
- Other causes: dilated cardiomyopathy, ARVC, sarcoidosis, Chagas disease
- 3D electroanatomic mapping identifies scar and critical isthmus
- VT often develops 10–15 years after myocardial infarction (slow progression of scar maturation)

## Wide Complex Tachycardia: VT vs. SVT with Aberrancy

| Feature                          | Favors VT                          | Favors SVT + Aberrancy             |
|----------------------------------|------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| VA dissociation                  | Yes — pathognomonic                | No (1:1 conduction)                 |
| Capture beats                    | Yes — pathognomonic                | Not present                         |
| Fusion beats                     | Yes — pathognomonic                | Not present                         |
| QRS duration                     | Often > 140 ms                     | Usually < 140 ms                    |
| QRS axis                         | Northwest axis common              | Usually normal or predictable axis  |
| History of MI / cardiomyopathy   | Strongly favors VT                 | Less likely VT                      |
| Prior normal EKG                 | Less specific                      | BBB morphology same as baseline     |

**Clinical rule: wide complex tachycardia = VT until proven otherwise**

## VA Dissociation

- Atria and ventricles beating at different rates and independently of each other
- Atrial rate (P waves) < ventricular rate (QRS complexes) — the opposite of heart block
- Signs on EKG:
  - **Dissociated P waves** marching through the tachycardia at a slower rate (may be subtle)
  - **Capture beat**: a sinus P wave arrives at the right moment, conducts through AV node → normal narrow QRS in the middle of VT
  - **Fusion beat**: sinus impulse and VT wavefront activate the ventricle simultaneously → intermediate QRS morphology (partly wide, partly normal)
- Presence of capture or fusion beats **proves VT** (since the AV node can still conduct when given the chance)

## Polymorphic VT and Ventricular Fibrillation

### Polymorphic VT
- QRS morphology changes from beat to beat
- Two main types:
  1. **Torsades de pointes (TdP)**: "twisting of the points"; undulating axis change; associated with **long QT interval**
  2. **Polymorphic VT from ischemia**: random QRS morphologies; associated with acute MI or ischemia

### Torsades de Pointes (TdP)
- Mechanism: **early afterdepolarization (EAD)** during phase 3 of the action potential triggers a PVC, which then initiates polymorphic VT
- Long-short-long pattern: short R-R followed by long R-R then the initiating PVC
- Twisting sinusoidal envelope visible on continuous monitor
- Causes:
  - Congenital long QT syndrome (LQTS)
  - Drug-induced QT prolongation (antiarrhythmics, antipsychotics, antibiotics, antifungals, methadone, antihistamines)
  - Electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia)
  - Combined drug-drug interactions in elderly (polypharmacy)
- Treatment: IV magnesium sulfate (first-line); correct electrolytes; stop offending drugs; overdrive pacing; ICD if congenital LQTS

### Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)
- Chaotic, completely disorganized ventricular activation
- No effective cardiac output → cardiac arrest → death within minutes if untreated
- EKG: irregular, rapid, small waveforms (no recognizable QRS)
- Patient is unresponsive and pulseless — requires immediate CPR + defibrillation
- Most common cause: acute myocardial ischemia/infarction
- Treatment: immediate defibrillation (unsynchronized shock); open the artery (PCI)

## Congenital Long QT Syndrome (LQTS)

- Mutations in ion channel genes (potassium or sodium channels)
- **Autosomal dominant** (Romano-Ward syndrome) or autosomal recessive + deafness (Jervell-Lange Nielsen)
- **Variable penetrance**: family members may carry the gene but have normal EKGs or minimal QT prolongation
- Common subtypes:
  - **LQT1**: KCNQ1 (IKs channel) — triggered by **exercise**, swimming; beta-blockers very effective
  - **LQT2**: KCNH2 (IKr channel) — triggered by **sudden noise/alarm/surprise**; avoid alarms, beta-blockers, ICD if high risk
  - **LQT3**: SCN5A (INa channel) — triggered during **sleep/rest**; mexiletine, ICD
- Symptoms: syncope, seizures, sudden death
- Risk of TdP → VF → sudden cardiac death
- Treatment: beta-blockers (LQT1/LQT2), ICD for survivors of cardiac arrest or high-risk patients

## Brugada Syndrome

- **SCN5A mutation** (sodium channel) — autosomal dominant
- EKG: **ST elevation ≥ 2 mm** in V1–V3 with characteristic coved (Type 1) morphology
- Type 1 (coved) pattern: obligate finding; saddleback pattern (Type 2) = lower risk
- Pattern may be **unmasked** by fever, alcohol, cocaine, antiarrhythmic drugs, metabolic abnormalities
- Sudden death during **sleep** (VF usually nocturnal)
- More common in **Southeast Asian males** (Laotian/Thai "sudden unexplained nocturnal death" syndrome SUDS/SUNDS); average first event age 41
- Treatment: ICD for high-risk patients; isoproterenol for electrical storm; quinidine (reduces recurrences)
- EKG normalization between events does not exclude diagnosis

## Arrhythmogenic RV Cardiomyopathy (ARVC) — Brief Mention

- Fibrofatty replacement of RV myocardium
- Epsilon wave in V1 (delayed RV activation)
- VT with LBBB morphology (from RV)
- Exercise can precipitate VT
- Familial; ICD indicated

## Pacemakers

### Indications
- Symptomatic bradycardia (sinus node dysfunction, high-degree AV block)
- Second-degree Mobitz II and third-degree heart block (regardless of symptoms, due to progression risk)
- Drug-induced bradycardia if drug cannot be stopped
- Tachy-brady syndrome (sick sinus syndrome with alternating tachycardia and bradycardia)

### Components
- **Battery/generator**: titanium can, implanted under the clavicle (subcutaneous pocket)
- **Pacing leads**: silicone/polyurethane-insulated wires; active fixation (helical screw) at the tip
- **Sensing**: bipolar electrode at tip detects intrinsic electrograms
- **Pacing**: delivers small electrical impulse (0.5–2 V, 0.5 ms pulse width) when no intrinsic beat detected

### Pacemaker Modes
- Single-chamber: atrial (AAI) for sinus node disease only; ventricular (VVI) for chronic AF with bradycardia
- Dual-chamber (DDD): senses and paces both atrium and ventricle; maintains AV synchrony
- Biventricular (CRT): paces RV + LV simultaneously for cardiac resynchronization in LBBB + heart failure
- His bundle pacing / left bundle branch area pacing: physiological pacing through native conduction system

### EKG Appearance
- Pacing spike (vertical line) immediately before the paced chamber
- Atrial paced: spike → P wave; normal QRS if intrinsic conduction intact
- Ventricular paced: spike → wide QRS (LBBB morphology if RV apex pacing)
- Pacemaker failure modes: oversensing, undersensing, loss of capture, lead fracture, infection

### Pacemaker Complications
- Lead fracture or insulation breach (over millions of heartbeats)
- Pacemaker infection (device pocket or lead endocarditis) — leads must be extracted
- Pacemaker syndrome (VVI pacing without atrial tracking → cannon A waves, lightheadedness)

## Defibrillators (ICD — Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator)

### Indications
- Secondary prevention: survivors of VF or hemodynamically unstable VT
- Primary prevention: EF ≤ 35% with heart failure (MADIT-II, SCD-HeFT trials)
- High-risk channelopathy patients (LQTS, Brugada, CPVT) after appropriate risk stratification
- ARVC with VT

### Preview (full lecture next session)
- Sense arrhythmia via intracardiac electrogram
- Deliver ATP (antitachycardia pacing) for slower VT → avoids shock
- High-voltage shock (20–40 joules) for VF or fast VT
- Subcutaneous ICD (S-ICD): no transvenous leads; no pacing capability; for primary prevention without pacing indication
