## Opening Case: Anterior MI with Conduction System Injury

- Wide QRS, loss of anterior R waves, prolonged PR interval, **northwest axis**
- Mechanism: LAD occlusion → anterior MI damages the conduction system (left bundle branch, proximal His bundle, or fascicles)
- Findings consistent with extensive anterior infarction with infranodal conduction disease

## SVT Review: Key Differentiating Features

### AVNRT
- **Short RP**: pseudo R' in V1, retrograde P just after QRS (< 70 ms)
- Dual AV nodal pathways

### Orthodromic AVRT
- **Short RP**: retrograde P visible in ST segment, but further from QRS than AVNRT
- Accessory pathway required (can localize from retrograde P-wave polarity)

### Atrial Tachycardia (AT)
- **Long RP**: P-wave is well separated from QRS (RP > PR)
- P-wave axis different from sinus P waves
- Negative P in inferior leads if ectopic focus low in RA or near CS
- Focal automatic arrhythmia — **NOT dependent on AV node**
- Adenosine causes AV block but does NOT terminate the tachycardia
- Heart rate typically 120–150 bpm
- Associated with structural heart disease, electrolyte abnormalities, drug toxicity
- Common sites: crista terminalis (RA), pulmonary vein ostia (LA)
- Harder to ablate (may stop with sedation)

## Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

### Definition and Characteristics
- **Irregularly irregular rhythm** with **no discernible P waves** (fibrillatory baseline)
- Atrial rate: 400–700 bpm (chaotic, disorganized activation)
- Ventricular rate: variable depending on AV nodal conduction; often 100–160 bpm if uncontrolled

### Mechanism: Two-Factor Model

1. **Trigger**: Premature beats (usually from **pulmonary vein myocardial sleeves** — cardiomyocytes extending 1–3 cm into pulmonary veins)
   - PV ectopy rapidly fires and initiates AF
   - Other triggers: posterior LA wall, SVC, coronary sinus

2. **Substrate**: Atrial fibrosis/remodeling that sustains AF once initiated
   - Fibrosis creates conduction heterogeneity → multiple reentry wavelets
   - More fibrosis → more persistent AF
   - **Utah MRI staging** of atrial fibrosis: Stage I (minimal) → Stage IV (extensive)

### Types of AF (by Duration)

| Type        | Definition                                    |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Paroxysmal  | Self-terminating episodes, typically < 7 days |
| Persistent  | Sustained > 7 days; requires cardioversion    |
| Long-standing persistent | > 12 months                      |
| Permanent   | Accepted by patient and physician; no rhythm control |

### Risk Factors for AF

- **Age** (most important; AF prevalence 10% in >80-year-olds)
- Hypertension
- Obesity
- Obstructive sleep apnea
- Valvular heart disease (especially mitral stenosis/regurgitation)
- Heart failure / cardiomyopathy
- Coronary artery disease
- Hyperthyroidism
- Alcohol (acute and chronic — "holiday heart syndrome")
- Post-cardiac surgery (post-op AF common ~25–30%)
- Diabetes

### Alcohol and AF
- Chronic alcohol use → atrial fibrosis → more persistent AF
- Australian study: moderate drinkers (>10 drinks/week) randomized to abstinence vs. control
  - Abstinence group: significant reduction in AF episodes
  - Suggests a reversible component to alcohol-induced atrial fibrosis (if caught early)
- Individual susceptibility varies; some patients have AF with even small amounts of alcohol

## Consequences of AF

1. **Palpitations / symptoms**: most common complaint; fatigue, dyspnea, reduced exercise tolerance
2. **Stroke risk**: loss of effective atrial contraction → stagnant blood in left atrial appendage (LAA) → thrombus → embolic stroke
   - LAA is the primary site of clot formation
3. **Loss of atrial contraction (atrial kick)**: reduces CO by ~20% at rest; catastrophic in patients with already impaired LV function
4. **Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy**: persistent rapid ventricular rate → LV dysfunction (reversible with rate control)

## Therapies for AF

### Rate Control
- Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil), digoxin
- Slows ventricular rate without restoring sinus rhythm
- Target: resting HR < 80 bpm, exercise HR < 110 bpm

### Rhythm Control
- **Cardioversion**: electrical DC shock to restore sinus rhythm; requires anticoagulation ≥ 3 weeks before or TEE to rule out LAA clot
- **Antiarrhythmic drugs**: flecainide, propafenone (no structural disease), amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide
  - Significant drug interactions and toxicities
  - Success rates < 50% long-term for persistent AF

### Catheter Ablation for AF

#### Goal: Pulmonary Vein Isolation (PVI)
- Electrically isolate all four pulmonary veins from the left atrium
- Block triggers from entering the atrium
- Creates circumferential lesions around the PV ostia

#### Traditional Method: Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA)
- Point-by-point energy delivery (cauterization, ~50–70°C)
- Long procedure (4+ hours)
- Risk of **atrial-esophageal fistula** (esophagus lies immediately posterior to LA) — rate 1:1000–1:10,000; often fatal if occurs
- Risk of pulmonary vein stenosis (5–10%)
- Risk of phrenic nerve injury (right phrenic nerve runs between RUPV and SVC)

#### New Method: Pulsed Field Ablation (PFA)
- **Electroporation**: DC shocks between multiple electrode combinations; kills cells via irreversible membrane poration
- **Non-thermal** (very little heat generated) → spares collateral structures (esophagus, phrenic nerve, coronary arteries)
- Multipolar basket/flower/sphere catheters → ablates entire PV in seconds
- Procedure time cut from 4 hours to 1–2 hours
- Success rates improved to ~85%+ (up from ~55% with old RF)
- FDA-approved in USA ~2024
- Limitation: near coronary arteries anteriorly, still some risk; radiofrequency still used anteriorly in some cases

#### Success Rates
- Traditional RF PVI: ~55% single-procedure success; majority needed redo procedures
- Modern PFA PVI: ~85% success; most patients need only one procedure

## Ventricular Arrhythmias: Introduction

(Continued in greater detail in subsequent lectures)

### Premature Ventricular Contractions (PVCs)
- Wide, bizarre QRS, no preceding P wave
- Very common (everyone has them)
- Usually originate from **outflow tract** (RVOT or LVOT) — "lost" pacemaker cells from cardiac development
- Most are benign; treatment not usually needed
- If >20% of beats are PVCs → may cause PVC-mediated cardiomyopathy
- Specific patterns: ventricular bigeminy (every other beat), trigeminy (every third beat)

### Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)
- Wide complex regular rhythm, rate 40–100 bpm (below tachycardia threshold)
- Commonly seen after myocardial reperfusion ("reperfusion arrhythmia") — benign
- No treatment required; indicates successful reperfusion

### Monomorphic vs. Polymorphic VT (Preview)
- **Monomorphic**: same QRS morphology each beat; reentry in scar tissue
- **Polymorphic**: changing QRS axis; associated with ischemia or long QT
