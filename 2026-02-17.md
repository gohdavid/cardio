## Overview

## Review: Venous Return + Ventricular Output Curves

- Cardiac output is determined by the **intersection** of two curves:
  1. **Venous return curve**: VR = (PMS – Pf) / R; decreasing function of right atrial pressure (Pf)
  2. **Cardiac output (ventricular output) curve**: increasing function of right atrial pressure up to a plateau
- At steady state, cardiac output = venous return (Pf is the operating point)

## Pulmonary Vasculature

### Anatomy
- Pulmonary arteries are thin-walled, highly compliant; can accommodate large volume changes without large pressure increases
- Low resistance (~1–2 Wood units), low pressure (~25/10 mmHg)
- High compliance allows the pulmonary circulation to act as a buffer between RV pulsatile output and smooth left atrial filling

### Pulmonary Vascular Resistance (PVR): Non-Linearity

PVR is not fixed; it decreases with increasing flow or increasing pressure via two mechanisms:
1. **Recruitment**: previously collapsed capillaries open as flow/pressure increases
2. **Distension**: already-open vessels enlarge in diameter → ↓resistance

This means PVR vs. flow is a hyperbolic/inverse curve — PVR falls as cardiac output rises. This protects against pulmonary hypertension during exercise.

## West Zones of the Lung

Gravitational effects create zones based on the relationship among alveolar pressure (PA), arterial pressure (Pa), and venous pressure (Pv):

| Zone | Condition              | Flow Pattern                    |
|------|------------------------|---------------------------------|
| I    | PA > Pa > Pv           | No flow (alveoli collapse vessels; rare except in low CO or PEEP) |
| II   | Pa > PA > Pv           | Flow determined by Pa – PA (Starling resistor / waterfall) |
| III  | Pa > Pv > PA           | Normal flow; determined by Pa – Pv |

- In an upright person: Zone I at apex, Zone III at base (most blood flow at base)
- Supine position: West zones equalize; more uniform perfusion
- Clinical relevance: in Zone I (no flow), there is dead-space ventilation but no gas exchange

## Heart-Lung Pumping Unit

The right ventricle (RV) and lungs function together as a unit transferring blood from the venous return to the left ventricle. This "heart-lung pumping unit" determines cardiac output.

**Three Regions of Cardiac Output:**

1. **Region 1 (RV-limited)**: Normal operating range. RV end-diastolic volume < maximum. Cardiac output increases with increasing venous return. CO determined mainly by right ventricular performance. (RV is the "third grader," LV is the "bodybuilder" — rate-limiting is the weaker RV.)

2. **Region 2 (RV volume-maximized / plateau)**: RV is maximally dilated; further increases in filling pressure do not increase CO. The RV is "saturated." CO is limited by maximum RV volume capacity.

3. **Region 3 (LV failure)**: Systemic aortic pressure exceeds maximal LV systolic pressure. LV failure limits CO. Loop extends off the right side on the PV loop.

**Under normal physiological conditions, we always operate in Region 1.**

## Effects of Intrathoracic Pressure on Cardiac Output

### Closed-chest (normal)
- Negative intrathoracic pressure during inspiration facilitates venous return by decreasing right atrial pressure
- Also decreases LV afterload (assists ejection)

### Mechanical Ventilation (positive pressure)
- Positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) raises intrathoracic pressure → compresses great veins → ↓venous return → ↓CO
- Also shifts venous return curve by raising Pf

### Tension Pneumothorax
- Massive positive intrathoracic pressure → near-total collapse of great veins → obstructive shock

---

## Autonomic Effects on Cardiac Output Curves

### Sympathetic Stimulation
- Increases heart rate and contractility → shifts cardiac output curve UP and LEFT
- Increases venous tone → raises PMS → shifts venous return curve RIGHT
- Net: higher operating CO and higher operating mean arterial pressure
- Full sympathetic stimulation can increase CO from ~5 to ~10+ L/min

### Parasympathetic (Vagal) Stimulation
- Primarily slows heart rate (M2 receptors on SA/AV nodes) → shifts cardiac output curve DOWN
- Minimal effect on vascular tone (parasympathetic fibers do not innervate veins significantly)

## Integrated Analysis: Clinical Scenarios

For each scenario: identify the initial perturbation to the venous return and/or cardiac output curves, then trace the new intersection.

### 1. Hemorrhage
- ↓Blood volume → ↓PMS → venous return curve shifts LEFT
- Initial: ↓CO, ↓MAP
- Compensation: sympathetic activation → ↑HR, ↑contractility, ↑venous tone (partial recovery of PMS)
- New steady state: lower CO than normal but partially compensated; tachycardia

### 2. Tilt / Orthostatic Stress
- Venous pooling in dependent circulation (legs) → effective ↑zero-pressure filling volume → ↓effective PMS
- Equivalent to acute blood loss
- Compensation: baroreceptor reflex → ↑HR, vasoconstriction

### 3. Arteriovenous Fistula (AVF)
- Creates low-resistance pathway → ↓SVR → ↓MAP
- ↓Resistance makes the venous return curve steeper; PMS is often treated as roughly unchanged initially
- ↑CO (Frank-Starling) but at the cost of lower MAP
- High-output heart failure in extreme cases

### 4. Acute MI
- ↓Contractility → cardiac output curve shifts DOWN
- ↓CO → ↓MAP → baroreceptors trigger compensation (↑HR, vasoconstriction, ↑venous tone)
- Compensated at lower CO, higher Pf (venous congestion)
- Cardiogenic shock if CO drops severely

### 5. Pneumothorax
- Raised intrathoracic pressure → compresses great veins → obstructive reduction in VR
- ↓CO despite normal myocardial function (obstructive, not cardiogenic)

## PV Loop Analysis for Step Changes

When cardiac output changes:
- Sudden increase in preload (volume loading): loop shifts right; wider loop (more SV)
- Sudden increase in afterload: loop shifts upward/left; narrower SV, higher ESV
- Sudden ↑ contractility: steeper ESPVR; same EDV → smaller ESV → larger SV

---

## Key Takeaways

- Cardiac output is determined by the intersection of venous return and cardiac output curves
- The lungs and heart act as a unit; RV performance is the normal rate-limiting factor
- Intrathoracic pressure directly affects venous return (inspiration helps, positive-pressure ventilation hinders)
- The three regions of CO define the boundaries of normal vs. RV-limited vs. LV-failure physiology
- All major clinical cardiovascular perturbations can be analyzed by their effect on PMS, resistance, HR, and contractility
