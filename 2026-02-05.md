## Overview

Fundamental hemodynamic relationships: Ohm's Law analogy, compliance, cardiac output measurement, Frank-Starling preload dependence, and the Fick principle.

---

## Ohm's Law Analogy for Blood Flow

Flow (Q), pressure difference (ΔP), and resistance (R) follow an analogous relationship to Ohm's Law:

> **Q = ΔP / R**

- Q = blood flow (L/min)
- ΔP = driving pressure (mmHg)
- R = vascular resistance

Rearranged: **R = ΔP / Q**

- Systemic vascular resistance (SVR) = (MAP – CVP) / CO ≈ 15–20 mmHg·min/L (Wood units) ≈ 1200–1600 dyn·s·cm⁻⁵
- Pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) = (MPAP – PCWP) / CO ≈ 1–2 Wood units
- Resistance is primarily regulated at the level of arterioles.

## Compliance

> **C = ΔV / ΔP**

- Compliance: the volume added per unit pressure rise.
- Arterial compliance (Ca) ≈ 2 mL/mmHg — stiff, high-pressure reservoir.
- Venous compliance (Cv) ≈ 100 mL/mmHg — highly compliant, low-pressure reservoir; veins contain ~70% of total blood volume.
- Compliance determines how volume changes translate to pressure changes in each compartment.

## Cardiac Output (CO)

> **CO = HR × SV**

- Normal resting CO ≈ 5 L/min
- Cardiac index (CI) = CO / BSA ≈ 2.5–4.0 L/min/m²
- Heart rate (HR): 60–100 bpm at rest
- Stroke volume (SV): ~70 mL at rest; determined by preload, afterload, and contractility

### Preload

- Preload = end-diastolic volume (EDV) or end-diastolic pressure (EDP), the stretch of the myocardium before contraction.
- Higher preload → longer sarcomere length → more cross-bridge formation → greater force of contraction (Frank-Starling mechanism).

### Afterload

- Afterload = the load opposing ejection; approximated by systolic wall stress (Laplace) or aortic impedance.
- Increased afterload → reduced SV unless compensated by increased contractility.

### Contractility (Inotropy)

- Intrinsic property of myocardium independent of preload and afterload.
- Increased by sympathetic stimulation (β₁ receptors), catecholamines, calcium sensitizers.
- Decreased by acidosis, hypoxia, negative inotropes (β-blockers, calcium channel blockers).

## Frank-Starling Mechanism

- The ventricular output curve (Starling curve) plots SV (or CO) against preload (EDV or filling pressure).
- As preload increases, CO increases — up to a maximum ("Starling's optimum").
- Heart failure shifts the curve downward and to the right.
- Clinical applications: Preload reduction (diuretics) and preload optimization (IV fluids) exploit this relationship.

## The Fick Principle

Cardiac output can be calculated from oxygen consumption and the arteriovenous oxygen difference:

> **CO = VO₂ / (CaO₂ – CvO₂)**

Where:
- VO₂ = whole-body oxygen consumption (~250 mL O₂/min at rest)
- CaO₂ = arterial oxygen content (~20 mL O₂/100 mL blood)
- CvO₂ = mixed venous oxygen content (~15 mL O₂/100 mL blood)
- Normal CO ≈ 250 / (200–150) = 5 L/min

### Using the Fick Principle Clinically

- Requires: inspired/expired gas analysis (VO₂ measurement) + simultaneous arterial and mixed venous blood gases.
- Mixed venous blood must come from pulmonary artery (right heart catheterization) for proper mixing.
- Increased VO₂ (exercise, fever) → higher CO required to maintain CaO₂ – CvO₂.
- Decreased CO (heart failure) → widened CaO₂ – CvO₂ (tissues extract more O₂ per unit blood).

## Ventricular Output Curve (Cardiac Output Curve)

- Plots cardiac output (y-axis) vs. right atrial pressure (x-axis) — "Guyton's cardiac function curve."
- Shape: rises steeply at low RAP; flattens at high RAP (afterload limitation or ventricular failure).
- Positive inotropy: shifts curve up and left.
- Negative inotropy: shifts curve down and right.

## Clinical Pearls

- In cardiogenic shock: CO↓, SVR↑ (compensatory vasoconstriction), widened AV O₂ difference.
- In septic shock: CO↑ initially, SVR↓↓, narrow AV O₂ difference.
- Fick principle assumes steady state; invalid during rapidly changing conditions.
