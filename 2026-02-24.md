## Review: Single Equivalent Moving Dipole

- The net sum of all active myocardial dipoles at any instant = single equivalent moving dipole **m(t)**
- ECG lead reading = projection of m onto the lead's orientation vector
- φ = (m · cos θ) / (4πσr²)
- Leads are simply different views (angular projections) of the same underlying cardiac electrical process

---

## Standard Limb Leads (Bipolar)

Leads I, II, III form Einthoven's triangle:

| Lead | Equation                         | Axis Angle |
|------|----------------------------------|------------|
| I    | Left arm (+) − Right arm (−)     | 0°         |
| II   | Left leg (+) − Right arm (−)     | +60°       |
| III  | Left leg (+) − Left arm (−)      | +120°      |

**Einthoven's Law**: Lead II = Lead I + Lead III (linear dependence; only 2 are independent)

---

## Augmented Limb Leads (Unipolar)

AVR, AVL, AVF are unipolar leads referenced to the average of the other two electrodes (Wilson Central Terminal):

| Lead | Positive pole | Reference             | Axis Angle |
|------|---------------|-----------------------|------------|
| AVR  | Right arm     | Average of LA + LL    | −150°      |
| AVL  | Left arm      | Average of RA + LL    | −30°       |
| AVF  | Left foot/leg | Average of RA + LA    | +90°       |

All six limb leads provide views in the **frontal plane** spaced 30° apart (covering 360°).

## The Alien Heart Exercise

A conceptual exercise demonstrating the dipole model: given a heart of unusual geometry (e.g., cylindrical), predict the ECG morphology in each lead.

- Apply the same physical principle: measure the projection of the net dipole on each lead vector
- For a cylindrical heart with unusual activation direction, the ECG can look completely different from normal
- This exercise reinforces that ECG waveforms are entirely determined by the direction and magnitude of the net dipole, not by the absolute shape of the waveform in any one lead

## Mean Electrical Axis

The mean electrical QRS axis = the average direction of the net QRS dipole during ventricular depolarization.

### Method for Determining Axis

1. Find the limb lead in which the QRS is most **isoelectric** (equal positive and negative deflections)
2. The mean electrical axis is **perpendicular** to that isoelectric lead
3. Check a perpendicular lead to determine which of the two perpendicular directions is positive

### Normal, Left, and Right Axis

| Axis Range       | Category              | Common Causes                              |
|------------------|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| −30° to +90°     | Normal                | —                                          |
| −30° to −90°     | Left axis deviation   | LVH, conduction abnormalities, infarction  |
| +90° to +180°    | Right axis deviation  | RVH, conduction abnormalities, abnormal cardiac orientation |
| −90° to +180°    | Extreme (NW) axis     | VT of LV origin, dextrocardia, lead error  |

- Lead I negative + AVF positive → right axis deviation (quadrant: right-lower)
- Lead I positive + AVF negative → left axis deviation (quadrant: left-upper)
- Lead I negative + AVF negative → extreme northwest axis

## Precordial (Chest) Leads V1–V6

The chest leads give projections in the **horizontal (transverse) plane** — orthogonal to the frontal plane information of limb leads.

| Lead | Position                                       | Views Primarily |
|------|------------------------------------------------|-----------------|
| V1   | 4th ICS, right sternal border                  | RV, septum       |
| V2   | 4th ICS, left sternal border                   | RV, septum       |
| V3   | Between V2 and V4                              | Anterior wall    |
| V4   | 5th ICS, midclavicular line                    | Anterior LV      |
| V5   | Anterior axillary line, same level as V4       | Lateral LV       |
| V6   | Midaxillary line, same level as V4             | Lateral LV       |

### Normal R-Wave Progression

- V1: small r wave, deep S wave (rS pattern) — activation moving away from V1
- V4: transition zone (RS pattern, equal R and S) — normal transition between V3–V4
- V6: tall R wave, small or absent S (qR or R pattern) — activation toward V6

**Poor R-wave progression**: loss of anterior R waves; seen with anterior MI, LVH, LBBB, or poor lead placement

**Early R-wave progression**: transition before V3; seen in posterior MI (posterior forces moving anteriorly toward V1)

## 12-Lead EKG Display Format

Standard 12-lead EKG is commonly displayed in 4 blocks of 3 leads, plus a rhythm strip:

```
Column 1     Column 2     Column 3     Column 4 (Rhythm Strip)
Lead I       Lead aVR     Lead V1      Lead II (full strip)
Lead II      Lead aVL     Lead V2
Lead III     Lead aVF     Lead V3      Lead V4
             [blank]      [blank]      Lead V5
             [blank]      [blank]      Lead V6
```

All 12 leads recorded **simultaneously**; the rhythm strip runs continuously at the bottom.

Paper speed: **25 mm/s**
- Small box: 1 mm wide = **40 ms**
- Large box: 5 mm wide = **200 ms**

---

## Heart Rate Calculation

Standard method using large boxes between R-R intervals:

| Large Boxes Between R–R | Heart Rate (bpm) |
|-------------------------|------------------|
| 1                        | 300              |
| 2                        | 150              |
| 3                        | 100              |
| 4                        | 75               |
| 5                        | 60               |
| 6                        | 50               |

Mnemonic: **300 – 150 – 100 – 75 – 60 – 50**

For irregular rhythms: count beats in 6-second strip (30 large boxes) × 10 = rate

## EKG Reading Algorithm

Systematic approach to reading a 12-lead EKG:

1. **QRS morphology**: narrow (<120 ms) vs. wide (≥120 ms)?
2. **P waves**: present? Morphology? Relationship to QRS?
3. **Rate**: calculate from R-R interval
4. **Rhythm**: regular? Irregular? Type of rhythm
5. **Axis**: calculate from limb leads
6. **Intervals**: PR, QRS, QT
7. **Atrial enlargement**: P-wave morphology in II and V1
8. **Ventricular hypertrophy**: voltage criteria in chest and limb leads
9. **ST/T changes**: ST elevation, depression, T-wave inversion
10. **Other**: Q waves, U waves, artifact

---

## Normal Sinus Rhythm Criteria

1. P wave before every QRS, QRS after every P wave
2. P wave positive in lead II, negative in AVR
3. PR interval 120–200 ms
4. QRS < 120 ms
5. Rate 60–100 bpm

**Sinus tachycardia**: same as NSR but rate > 100 bpm
**Sinus bradycardia**: same as NSR but rate < 60 bpm

## Vectorcardiography (Brief Mention)

- Vectorcardiogram plots the tip of the QRS vector over time as a loop in 3D space
- More complete spatial representation than scalar ECG leads
- Clinically largely obsolete; replaced by standard 12-lead plus imaging
- Still useful conceptually for understanding spatial orientation of cardiac electrical forces

---

## Ladder Diagrams

A graphical tool representing cardiac conduction:
- Horizontal rows: SA node, atria, AV node, ventricles
- Diagonal lines show timing and pathway of electrical conduction
- Lines that stop indicate conduction block
- Used for analysis of complex arrhythmias and heart blocks
