## Feedback Control System Framework

The cardiovascular system is a **feedback control system**:

- **Set point**: target arterial blood pressure (~93 mmHg MAP)
- **Sensor**: baroreceptors (carotid sinus, aortic arch)
- **Error signal**: difference between sensed pressure and set point
- **Controller**: brainstem cardiovascular control center (NTS, RVLM, DVN)
- **Effectors**: heart (rate + contractility), blood vessels (resistance + venous tone)
- **Output**: actual arterial blood pressure

## Autonomic Nervous System

### Parasympathetic (Vagal, "Rest and Digest")
- **Cranial nerve X (vagus)**: main output to heart
- **Neurotransmitter**: acetylcholine (ACh) at both ganglia and target organs
- **Receptor**: M2 muscarinic receptors on SA node and AV node
- **Effects on heart**: ↓HR (negative chronotropy), ↓AV conduction velocity
- Minimal effect on ventricular contractility
- **Very little effect on peripheral vasculature** (except some specialized territories)
- **Onset/offset**: extremely rapid (seconds) — can slow heart within one beat
- **Vagal arrest**: extreme vagal stimulation → transient SA node arrest → junctional escape beat

### Sympathetic ("Fight or Flight")
- **Thoracolumbar outflow** (T1–L2)
- **Preganglionic**: ACh; **postganglionic**: norepinephrine (NE) to most organs; epinephrine (E) from adrenal medulla
- **Onset**: ~15–20 seconds after maximal stimulation
- **Offset**: prolonged (NE persists longer due to reuptake and slow metabolism)

## Adrenergic Receptor Subtypes and Effects

| Receptor | Location                    | Agonist         | Effect                                    |
|----------|-----------------------------|-----------------|-------------------------------------------|
| α₁       | Vascular smooth muscle (arteries) | NE, E (high) | Vasoconstriction; ↑SVR; ↑PMS (venoconstriction) |
| α₂       | Presynaptic, CNS            | NE              | Feedback inhibition of NE release         |
| β₁       | Heart (SA, AV, ventricles)  | NE, E           | ↑HR, ↑contractility, ↑conduction velocity |
| β₂       | Peripheral SM, bronchi      | E (low dose)    | Vasodilation (skeletal muscle), bronchodilation |

**Epinephrine dose-dependence**:
- Low dose: β₂ predominant → vasodilation (useful in anaphylaxis to reverse bronchospasm)
- High dose: α₁ predominant → vasoconstriction (EpiPen effect in anaphylaxis: high-dose push → constriction, ↑BP)

## Sympathetic Effects on Cardiovascular Control Curves

### Effect on Venous Return Curve
1. **↑Venous tone** (α₁ venoconstriction) → ↓zero-pressure filling volume → **↑PMS** → curve shifts rightward (x-intercept increases)
2. **↑Resistance to venous return** → shallower VR slope (less flow for a given pressure gradient)

### Effect on Cardiac Output (Heart-Lung Pumping Unit) Curve
1. **↑Heart rate** (β₁) → steeper slope of CO curve
2. **↑Contractility** (β₁ → ↑Ca²⁺ release, phospholamban phosphorylation) → steeper ESPVR slope + leftward offset → CO curve shifts up and left

### Net Effect of Full Sympathetic Activation
- Normal resting CO ~5 L/min → maximal sympathetic stimulation → **CO ~10–12 L/min**
- Blood pressure maintained or increased
- Both curves shift: new intersection at higher CO and similar or higher mean arterial pressure

### Parasympathetic Effect
- Primarily affects HR (M2 on SA node) → shifts CO curve down
- Minimal effect on venous return curve

## Push-Pull (Seesaw) of Heart Rate

- At any moment: HR = resultant of sympathetic tone (↑HR via β₁) and vagal tone (↓HR via M2)
- At rest: vagal tone predominates → resting HR typically 60–75 bpm (lower than intrinsic SA rate ~90–100)
- **Heart rate variability (HRV)**: fluctuations in beat-to-beat interval; high HRV = healthy autonomic tone; low HRV after MI → increased mortality risk

## Baroreceptors (Arterial Baroreflex)

### Location
1. **Carotid sinus**: at the bifurcation of the common carotid artery (internal carotid); most sensitive
2. **Aortic arch**: stretch receptors in the aortic wall; less sensitive but similar function

### Physiology
- **Stretch receptors** in the vessel wall
- **Firing rate** ∝ mean pressure AND rate of pressure change (dynamic response)
- At higher blood pressure: more stretch → higher firing rate → sends inhibitory signal to sympathetic center → ↓HR, ↓SVR → BP falls back toward set point
- At lower blood pressure: less stretch → reduced firing → ↑sympathetic outflow → ↑HR, ↑SVR, ↑venous tone → BP rises

### Clinical Examples
- **Carotid sinus massage**: manual compression → ↑firing → reflex bradycardia (used to terminate AVNRT, diagnose carotid sinus hypersensitivity)
- **Carotid sinus syncope**: elderly patients; very sensitive baroreceptors; minor stimulation (tight collar, head turning) → reflex bradycardia/hypotension → syncope
- **Baroreceptor resetting**: in chronic hypertension, baroreceptors reset to a new set point → defend the elevated pressure

## Chemoreceptors

### Peripheral Chemoreceptors
- **Carotid body** (at carotid bifurcation, adjacent to carotid sinus)
- **Aortic bodies** (in aortic arch region)
- Sense: ↓PaO₂ (most sensitive), ↑PaCO₂, ↓pH
- Response: ↑ventilation + ↑sympathetic tone → ↑HR, ↑BP, vasoconstriction

### Central Chemoreceptors
- In medulla (near NTS)
- Primarily sense ↑PCO₂/↓pH in CSF
- Primarily regulate respiration but interact with cardiovascular control

---

## Cardiac Stretch Receptors

- Located at **junction of pulmonary veins and LA** (atrial mechanoreceptors)
- Also in ventricular walls and RV
- Sense: ↑filling → activates Bainbridge reflex (↑HR with volume loading)
- Also regulate ANP/BNP release (atrial natriuretic peptide/brain natriuretic peptide)
- Input to vagal afferents → volume regulation signal to kidneys (↑ANP → ↑natriuresis → ↓volume)

## Vascular Smooth Muscle Autoregulation

### Myogenic Response (Endothelium-Independent)
- Smooth muscle responds to **transmural pressure** (stretch)
- ↑Intraluminal pressure → mechanical stretch of smooth muscle → ↑Ca²⁺ entry through stretch-activated channels → contraction → ↓diameter (paradoxical: vessel constricts when stretched)
- Endothelium-denuded vessels still show this response (endothelium-independent)
- Functionally: prevents over-distension and helps maintain constant blood flow despite pressure changes

### Flow-Mediated (Endothelium-Dependent) Vasodilation
- ↑Blood flow → ↑shear stress on endothelium → endothelial cells detect via mechanosensors
- **Releases nitric oxide (NO)** → vascular smooth muscle relaxation → ↓resistance → ↑diameter
- Removing the endothelium abolishes this response (endothelium-dependent)
- Nobel Prize 1998: discovery of NO as EDRF (endothelium-derived relaxing factor)

### Experiment Demonstrating Both Mechanisms
- Isolated vessel in flow chamber:
  - ↑Transmural pressure (stretch) with constant flow → **vessel constricts** (myogenic, endothelium-independent)
  - ↑Perfusion pressure (flow) with constant transmural pressure → **vessel dilates** (endothelium-dependent NO release)
  - Adding nitric oxide inhibitor abolishes the dilation response

### Key Vasoactive Agents

| Agent              | Source                  | Effect on Vessel          |
|--------------------|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| Norepinephrine     | Sympathetic terminals   | Constriction (α₁)         |
| Epinephrine (low)  | Adrenal medulla         | Dilation (β₂)             |
| Epinephrine (high) | Adrenal medulla         | Constriction (α₁)         |
| Angiotensin II     | RAAS                    | Constriction              |
| Endothelin-1       | Endothelium             | Constriction              |
| Nitric oxide (NO)  | Endothelium             | Dilation (cGMP)           |
| Prostacyclin (PGI₂)| Endothelium             | Dilation                  |
| Adenosine          | Local tissue metabolism | Dilation                  |
| CO₂/H⁺            | Metabolic activity      | Dilation                  |
| Low O₂             | Tissue hypoxia          | Dilation (systemic)       |
| Acetylcholine      | Parasympathetic         | Dilation (via NO release) |

## Autoregulation: Pressure-Flow Relationship

- In a **passive tube**: ↑perfusion pressure → ↑flow proportionally
- In a **living vessel** with autoregulation: flow returns toward baseline despite ↑perfusion pressure (over 20–30 seconds)
- This protects downstream tissues from pressure surges
- **Autoregulatory range**: the range of pressures over which flow is maintained constant
  - Brain: 60–160 mmHg
  - Kidney: 80–180 mmHg
- Outside the range: **pressure-passive** — flow tracks pressure

## Tilt (Orthostatic Stress) Analysis with Curves

### Uncontrolled State (No Compensatory Reflex)
- Upright tilt → venous pooling in legs → ↑zero-pressure filling volume → ↓PMS
- VR curve shifts leftward (equivalent to hemorrhage)
- Cardiac output curve unchanged (heart rate and contractility unchanged without reflex)
- New intersection: ↓CO, ↓MAP

### With Autonomic Compensation
- ↓BP detected by baroreceptors → ↑sympathetic outflow + ↓vagal tone
- Effects:
  - ↑HR, ↑contractility → cardiac output curve shifts up
  - ↑Venous tone → ↑PMS → VR curve shifts right
  - ↑Arteriolar constriction helps support MAP, but if resistance to venous return rises the VR slope becomes shallower
- New steady state: CO and MAP partially (but not fully) restored toward normal
- **No overcompensation**: CO never exceeds the pre-tilt baseline under pure reflex compensation

### Autonomic Failure
- In patients with autonomic neuropathy: no HR change, no TPR change with tilt
- BP drops dramatically (50% CO drop, 50% SV drop)
- No heart rate compensation
- Management: graduated posture changes, compression stockings, mineralocorticoids

## Valsalva Maneuver

- Forced expiration against a closed glottis → ↑intrathoracic pressure
- **Phase I**: Increase in intrathoracic pressure → immediately transmitted to aorta → **transient BP increase** → reflex bradycardia
- **Phase II**: Sustained high intrathoracic pressure → collapses great veins → ↓VR → ↓SV, ↓pulse pressure → **↑HR** (compensatory tachycardia)
- **Phase III**: Release of intrathoracic pressure → immediate BP drop (blood redistributes)
- **Phase IV**: Rush of venous return → ↑preload → ↑SV → **BP overshoot** + reflex bradycardia

### Clinical Uses
- Terminate SVT: Phase II increases vagal tone → can break AVNRT/AVRT
- Diagnose autonomic failure: blunted HR response to Valsalva
- Assess baroreceptor sensitivity

## Exercise Cardiovascular Response (Overview)

1. **Immediate** (mechanical): abdominal muscle contraction squeezes splanchnic veins → ↑PMS → ↑VR → passive Starling increase in CO
2. **Early** (~15–20s): sympathetic activation → ↑HR, ↑contractility, ↑venous tone
3. **Steady state**: resistance to exercising muscles falls (metabolic vasodilation); resistance elsewhere increases; CO 4–6× resting; BP ↑ slightly (exercise hypertension)
4. **Maximal exercise**: all exercising muscle vasodilature maximally dilated; CO cannot increase further without ↑BP

Cardiac output curve progression:
- A (rest) → B (mechanical squeeze of abdomen) → C (sympathetic + vasodilation) → D (maximal exercise)
