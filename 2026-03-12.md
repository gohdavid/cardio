## EKG Reading Approach (Rapid-Fire Cases)

Reminder of the systematic approach:
1. Rate → 2. Rhythm → 3. Axis → 4. Intervals → 5. P-wave morphology → 6. Hypertrophy → 7. ST/T changes → 8. Other findings

## Case 1: Junctional Rhythm / Sinus Arrest

- No visible P waves; narrow QRS; regular; rate ~40 bpm
- **Junctional rhythm**: escape pacemaker from AV junction
- Narrow QRS because conduction through bundle branches is normal
- Retrograde P waves (inverted in inferior leads) may or may not be visible depending on timing
- Differential: complete sinus arrest vs. junctional rhythm suppressing sinus node via retrograde VA conduction
- **Escape vs. PVC distinction**: escape beats are intrinsic backup pacemakers firing because there is no heartbeat; PVCs are premature (abnormal) beats that fire early
- Escape = protective; PVC = ectopic (abnormal automaticity or triggered activity)

### Clinical Context
- Causes: drug toxicity (β-blockers, CCBs, digoxin), sick sinus syndrome, dialysis patients with missed sessions (hyperkalemia), hypothyroidism
- Treatment if symptomatic: remove offending drug; if persistent → permanent pacemaker

## Case 2: Parkinson's Disease Tremor — EKG Artifact

- Patient with Parkinson's disease; "sawtooth" baseline in several limb leads
- **Mimics atrial flutter** in limb leads (II, III, AVF appear to have regular 300 bpm activity)
- Key differentiator: look at **V1 and V2** — these leads are relatively still during tremor (chest wall movement minimal at the sternum)
- In V2: **clear P waves with normal rhythm visible; no flutter waves**
- The limb lead "flutter waves" = **tremor artifact** transmitted through the arm electrode leads
- Teaching point: **always look at multiple leads** to identify artifact; do not call atrial flutter based on one limb lead alone
- Other artifact sources: patient movement, poor electrode contact, nearby electrical equipment, respiratory motion

## Case 3: Young Normal Volunteer Post-Seizure

- Patient who just had a seizure: massive catecholamine release
- **Sinus tachycardia** at ~120 bpm: normal P waves in lead II, long RP tachycardia
- Normal axis, normal intervals, normal QRS
- Slightly unusual P wave morphology (taller than usual) — normal with tachycardia (SA node fires from a slightly higher location at fast rates)
- Subtle borderline left atrial enlargement signal in V1 — not diagnostic; tachycardia-related
- Important differential: always evaluate **cause of sinus tachycardia**
  - Common causes: hypoxia, hypovolemia, fever, pain, anxiety, thyrotoxicosis, PE
  - Post-seizure tachycardia: physiologic; resolves spontaneously

## Case 4: Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia

- Slightly irregular rhythm in a young person; rate variability correlates with respiration
- **Respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA)**: heart rate speeds up with inspiration, slows with expiration
- Mechanism: inspiratory reduction in vagal tone → ↑HR; expiratory restoration of vagal tone → ↓HR
- Normal finding, especially in young, physically fit individuals
- P-wave morphology consistent throughout (all sinus; excludes wandering atrial pacemaker)
- **Wandering atrial pacemaker**: ≥3 different P-wave morphologies; benign but pathological in origin

## Case 5: RSR' Pattern in V1 — Crochetage / Normal Variant vs. Epsilon Wave

- Young person with subtle terminal positive notch (R') in V1
- QRS duration normal (<120 ms) → does NOT meet criteria for complete RBBB
- **Crochetage pattern (incomplete RBBB pattern)**: normal variant related to sequential activation of bundle branches
- Can be normal; however, this terminal notch in V1 can also represent an **epsilon wave** — a marker of arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC)
- **Epsilon wave**: small positive deflection after the QRS in V1 or adjacent leads; represents delayed right ventricular depolarization from fibrofatty replacement
- Correct reporting: "rSR' in V1, normal QRS duration — possible normal variant; **consider myocardial disease in appropriate clinical context** (e.g., family history of ARVC, palpitations, syncope)"

## Case 6: QT Prolongation and 2:1 Block

- Anticonvulsant drug trial screening EKG
- **Very prolonged QTc** (~600 ms)
- **2:1 AV block** secondary to QT prolongation: repolarization period so long that the next P wave arrives during the ARP, cannot conduct
- Risk: early afterdepolarizations → **torsades de pointes** (polymorphic VT from long QT)
- Teaching point: if QT is so long that the next sinus P wave falls within the T wave, 2:1 block can develop — **functional (rate-related) AV block from long QT**, not true AV nodal disease

## Case 7: Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB) with Cardiomyopathy

- Wide QRS (~180 ms) — severely prolonged
- **LBBB morphology**: QS or broad negative in V1; broad, notched R wave in V6 (but in this case V6 not tall)
- Patient has severe dilated cardiomyopathy: both ventricles enlarged
- Loss of the tall R wave in V6 (expected in LBBB): due to disease of the LV — insufficient viable myocardium to generate high voltage
- EKG read: "Wide QRS, LBBB morphology; low lateral voltage suggesting severe LV cardiomyopathy"
- LBBB alone typically: left axis deviation + negative QRS in V1 + tall R in V6

## Case 8: Wandering Atrial Pacemaker vs. RSA

- Irregular rate; P waves visible but slightly varying morphology
- Most P waves look similar (likely RSA), but occasional P wave from slightly different site on crista terminalis
- Differentiating RSA from wandering atrial pacemaker:
  - RSA: P waves are morphologically similar; irregularity is rate-related
  - WAP: P waves have ≥3 distinct morphologies; irregularity from changing pacemaker site

## Case 9: WPW Delta Wave

- Young woman with palpitations
- **Delta wave**: slurring of initial QRS upstroke visible in multiple leads
- Short PR interval (< 120 ms)
- The delta wave creates a positive deflection in inferior and lateral leads
- Must distinguish delta wave from **ectopic atrial P waves merged with QRS** (pseudo-delta)
  - True delta: seen in all beats uniformly; PR is short; present even without tachycardia
  - Look for an isoelectric PR segment — if the PR segment is truly isoelectric, that argues against pre-excitation
- WPW diagnosis → EPS and ablation discussion

## Case 10: Ventricular Trigeminy

- Every third beat is a PVC (1–1–PVC–1–1–PVC pattern)
- Wide bizarre QRS for the PVC, no preceding P wave
- PVCs arise from outflow tract region

## Case 11: Atrial Flutter (with AV Block Revealed After Drug)

- Initial presentation: regular tachycardia at 150 bpm (2:1 flutter)
- After AV nodal blocking agent: heart rate decreases to 75 bpm (4:1 block)
- **Sawtooth flutter waves** now clearly visible in V1 and inferior leads
- Atrial flutter rate: ~300 bpm; flutter waves inverted in II, III, AVF (typical counterclockwise flutter)
- Teaching point: when ventricular rate is precisely 150 bpm, always suspect atrial flutter with 2:1 block

## Case 12: Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB)

- **Marked left axis deviation** (approximately −60°)
- QRS slightly wide (~110 ms) but < 120 ms — not full LBBB
- Small q in I, AVL; small r in II, III, AVF
- Accompanying first-degree AV block (PR slightly prolonged)
- Diagnosis: LAFB + 1st degree AV block = conduction system disease; may be described clinically as combined fascicular and AV nodal delay

## Case 13: Exercise Test EKG — Moderator Band Tachycardia

- Exercise treadmill test: Bruce protocol (progressively faster and steeper)
- During exercise: baseline sinus rhythm normal
- During peak exercise: PAC (premature atrial contraction) visible on T wave → conducts normally
- Shortly after: **wide complex tachycardia** with LBBB morphology
- Diagnosis: **Moderator band tachycardia** (rare, exercise-induced VT from right ventricular moderator band)
  - Moderator band: muscular structure in RV containing terminal right bundle fibers
  - With RV stress, develops pacemaker activity → left bundle branch block morphology (comes from RV, activates LV last)
  - Inferiorly directed (positive in II, III, AVF) because origin is at mid-to-apical RV
- Distinguished from LBBB SVT by exercise induction and morphology

---

## Case 14: Wide Complex Tachycardia — VT with Fusion Beat

- Regular wide complex tachycardia; bisects into VT vs. SVT with aberrancy differential
- **Key finding**: one beat that is narrower and slightly different morphology than the wide beats
  - Preceded by a P wave
  - Represents a **fusion beat**: sinus impulse partially activates the ventricle simultaneously with the VT circuit
- **Diagnosis: Ventricular tachycardia** — confirmed by fusion/capture beat
- VT localization: negative in I and AVL (leftward structure), coming from high in the heart → likely mitral annular / aortic-mitral continuity origin

## Teaching Points from Practice Session

1. Always use multiple leads; artifact in one may be clear sinus rhythm in another
2. Narrow QRS + no P waves = junctional; wide QRS + no P waves in tachycardia = assume VT until proven otherwise
3. The "fusion beat" is pathognomonic for VT when seen in a wide-complex tachycardia
4. RSR' in V1 with normal QRS duration = possible normal variant; ask about family history (ARVC, channelopathy)
5. QT interval: always correct for rate; extreme prolongation can cause 2:1 block
6. Delta waves: always look for them in young patients with palpitations or syncope
