## Overview

Introduction to the cardiovascular system through anatomy and fundamental physical laws governing cardiac wall stress.

---

## Cardiac Anatomy Review

- The heart is a four-chambered pump: right atrium (RA) → right ventricle (RV) → pulmonary circulation → left atrium (LA) → left ventricle (LV) → systemic circulation.
- The right heart is a low-pressure, high-compliance pump; the left heart is a high-pressure, thick-walled pump.
- Valves (tricuspid, pulmonic, mitral, aortic) enforce unidirectional flow.
- Coronary arteries arise from the aortic root just above the aortic valve; left main divides into LAD and circumflex; right coronary artery (RCA) supplies the right heart and inferior wall.

## Normal Cardiac Pressures

| Chamber / Vessel       | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|------------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Aorta / systemic art.  | ~120            | ~80              |
| Left ventricle         | ~120            | ~10              |
| Left atrium (PCWP)     | ~8–12           | —                |
| Pulmonary artery       | ~25             | ~10              |
| Right ventricle        | ~25             | ~5               |
| Right atrium (CVP)     | ~5              | —                |

- The ratio of LV to RV systolic pressure (~5:1) reflects the difference in afterload (systemic vs. pulmonary vascular resistance).
- Mean pulmonary arterial pressure (MPAP) ≈ 15 mmHg; pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) ≈ 1–2 Wood units, far less than systemic vascular resistance (SVR) ≈ 15–20 Wood units.

## Laplace's Law

The key relationship between wall tension (T), pressure (P), radius (r), and wall thickness (h):

> **T = P · r / (2h)**

- Wall tension rises with increasing chamber radius and transmural pressure.
- Wall tension falls with increasing wall thickness.
- Clinical implications:
  - A dilated, thin-walled ventricle (heart failure) has very high wall tension → increased myocardial oxygen demand.
  - A hypertrophied ventricle (thicker walls) normalizes tension at the cost of reduced compliance.
  - An aneurysm (enlarged radius, thin wall) is at risk for rupture because wall tension is disproportionately high.
- Laplace's Law also explains why a small aortic root has lower wall stress than a large aneurysmal aorta.

## Myocardial Anisotropy

- The myocardium is anisotropic: fibers run in helical layers from epicardium to endocardium.
- Fiber orientation changes across the wall (~60° at epicardium → 0° mid-wall → –60° at endocardium).
- This arrangement allows the LV to produce both longitudinal shortening and circumferential compression during systole, giving it efficient torsional (wringing) motion.
- Simple isotropic models underestimate actual mechanical complexity.

## Oxygen Delivery

- Myocardial oxygen consumption (MVO₂) is the primary determinant of coronary blood flow demand.
- Major determinants of MVO₂: heart rate, contractility (inotropy), and wall tension (which scales with afterload and preload via Laplace's Law).
- Coronary blood flow occurs predominantly in diastole; systolic compression nearly occludes coronary vessels.

## Fetal Circulation

- Fetal circulation features right-to-left shunts that bypass the non-functional fetal lungs:
  - **Foramen ovale**: shunts oxygenated blood from RA → LA.
  - **Ductus arteriosus**: shunts blood from pulmonary artery → aorta.
  - **Ductus venosus**: bypasses hepatic circulation.
- After birth, increased pulmonary blood flow and rising systemic oxygen levels cause closure of these shunts.
- Failure to close → congenital heart disease (ASD, patent ductus arteriosus).

## Key Equations

- Laplace's Law: **T = P · r / (2h)**
- Cardiac Output: **CO = HR × SV**
- Oxygen delivery: **DO₂ = CO × CaO₂**

---

## Clinical Pearls

- Dilated cardiomyopathy worsens wall stress via increasing r → oxygen demand outpaces supply → further dilation.
- Aortic stenosis increases afterload → concentric hypertrophy (↑h) → normalizes T but impairs diastolic filling.
- Pulmonary hypertension chronically loads the RV → RV hypertrophy → eventually RV failure.
